Chapter 1 - Evergreen

Dahrian remembered the rain. It was a sort of pour that seemed to seep into your bones and cut your skin with its cold fingers. No matter where you went, who you stood behind, you were still left shivering and wet on the cobblestone pavements.

She remembered how she had kept her head down to avoid the icy wind stinging her eyes.

She remembered peering out of the dark ringlets of her damp hair, only making out the base of the stand where a man stood. She knew that he was important somehow. High in rank. Much higher than her parents’.

She remembered her mother’s hand in her right hand and her father’s in her left as the man spoke words and phrases that Dahrian couldn’t exactly understand. She was only a young girl after all. But at the time, she did understand what it meant for her. She couldn’t live in Zephyrus anymore. After six years of famine, droughts, and other misfortunes on the aetherins, they wanted her gone. All because her hair was black instead of white.

Bad omen, words would whisper in marble hallways, where they thought Dahrian could not hear.

A jinx.

A cursed child.

They called her many things in those hallways, and no matter how hard she tried to block them out or clamp her wings to the side of her head, those cruel words crawled their way into her young mind. Scraping. Scratching. Clawing.

And here they were again, all spoken aloud right in front of Dahrian. She deserved to be exiled.

She remembered her mother crying. She sounded more like a wounded bird than a person.

She remembered her father pulling her close and telling her something important that she should never forget.

Except she did.

She remembered her mother cupping her face with her pale, milky hands and saying her name over and over again, tears flowing from her golden lashes.

But Dahrian couldn’t remember her face. Or her father’s face, for that matter. Or anything else from her childhood.

It was all just… gone.

Dahrian Brown opened her eyes, and the rain vanished. The wooden ceiling stared back at her, obscured in the shadows of early morning.

Good morning, ceiling. Don’t mind me, I’m just having the same bad dream I’ve had for the last twelve years.

After her heart stopped thudding and she could breathe somewhat normally again, she sat up in her bed. Turning towards the window, she viewed a fresh blanket of snow upon the side yard of the Brown manor. She figured she had about an hour to spare before the town of Warmhaven would officially start the day. She rubbed her eyes, already dreading what the new day would bring.

Hopefully not laundry.

Her winged ears ached, and she touched one subconsciously. She probably slept on them funny. She combed her fingers through them, soothing any ruffled feathers. And after about five minutes of contemplating everything she had ever experienced or done in her whole eighteen years of living, she stood up and began to get ready for the day.

Her room was small, but it never bothered her. Her bed sat in the back corner, right next to the window. A small desk with an oil lamp stood adjacent to it, and a wardrobe stood opposite of it. On the far wall, near the door, she had a large mirror that nearly touched the ground. She pulled a few items of clothing from the wardrobe and began to change. She caught her reflection in the mirror. She crossed to it, feeling strangely drawn to the glass, her feet cold against the stone floor. She touched her visage, looking for her parents’ features. Her father’s nose, maybe? Perhaps her mother’s curls? She raked her fingers through her unruly hair and sighed. She turned away from her image, shame darkening her heart.

Aetherins are a very superstitious race of people, and they saw Dahrian’s presence as a foul omen. So, when she was around six years old, the council members decided to kick her out, blaming all the bad things happening on her dark hair. She doesn’t remember much of her childhood before that, just the long carriage ride to the nearest human city, where they dumped her like a discarded dirty sock, where the Brown family took her in and raised her as one of their own for some mysterious reason.

Dahrian assumed it was bribery.

Growing up in Warmhaven as an aetherin wasn’t too much of a challenge to young Dahrian, but many of the human children thought she was too strange to play with and often rejected her. This didn’t bother Dahrian, however. She was always quite antisocial, and she preferred to play alone. She somehow did end up making a single friend as she grew. Her mother’s previous lady-in-waiting, Tessa Greymourne, was her first friend, and her only friend. Tess now worked as a blacksmith in town, crafting armour, weapons, and tools, not only for the citizens of the hold, but also for the armies of Oclen.

Dahrian pulled the gown over her chemise and looked outside again. The snow glittered back at her. After a moment, she aptly put on a coat. She didn’t want to catch her death out there. Just as she was putting on her stockings and lacing up her shoes, voices spoke outside her door.

She recognised them instantly. Her adoptive mother, Lady Beatrice, and the manor’s maid, Claire. They spoke in hushed tones as if they believed that Dahrian was still sound asleep and definitely not crouched with her ear to the door.

“...Nice family and a very wealthy one for that matter,” Claire whispered.

Dahrian had a few guesses for what they were planning. None of them were pleasant or involved her opinion, for that matter.

“The family could use more money… And Dahrian could use a little real control over her life. She spends too much time with that damn sword or with Tessa. She’s almost a spinster! We should’ve married her off when she was fourteen or fifteen. We should’ve never put it off for this long.”

She heard her mother pacing the floor. “I just hope Dahrian doesn’t object to this marriage…”

Dahrian yanked the door open, almost tearing it off its old hinges. She stood in the doorway, eyes blazing, and most certainly, ready to object.

“Marriage?! Father, how could you agree to such a thing?” Cyrus Grenwood demanded, forcefully putting down the knife he was sharpening before he accidentally stabbed himself with it. It had become rather dull from his recent whittling hobby, and it had been in need of a proper blade for quite some time, but that wasn’t the point. He could not be getting married.

“What unheavenly powers hath usurped thee? Cyrus, you simply cannot flee your filial duties.” His father pinched the bridge of his nose with an exasperated sigh. “We have contended this a myriad of times. Every young lad must take on a wife and grow the family name. And now it is your turn. You hath seen your cousin, yes? Markus? He got married last year, and he’s doing quite splendidly.”

“Markus has no ambition! I want to be an explorer, father, not a husband! I simply cannot traverse the Glaucous peaks with an ailing wife and a crying baby,” He reasoned. His father had tried to find him a wife ever since he was sixteen, and he did everything in his power to convince the bride’s parents that he would be a stain on their family name. From forging letters to releasing a honey badger into a courtroom, he’d done it all. Though it now seems that all these efforts over the last five years have failed.

The reason for this tomfoolery was simple.

Cyrus does not have the time for a wife or a family. He has too many hobbies to master, too many skills to learn, and far too many places to see. As a young lad, Cyrus was a very inquisitive child, always asking, wondering, and imagining how the world around him worked. He climbed trees to scribble little pictures of the clouds, collected rocks (still on his bookshelf), and read every single bloody book in the Grenwood family library before he was ten years old. If you had asked young Cyrus what effervescent meant, he would’ve ruffled the back of his fluffy blonde head and given you the definition. And the spelling. And the country of origin. (It was Clayor.) He wasn’t exactly the favorite of the Grenwood children.

A family would simply get in the way of his insatiable curiosity.

“You will be married to Lady Dahrian! And that is the last I will hear of it! I will not have you sabotaging this arrangement, young man.”

Cyrus felt the floor tilt beneath his boots.

“Lady Dahrian? Lady Dahrian Brown?”

“Yes, the Browns offered quite a large dowry for her, and I’m best chaps with Lord Brown, so it works out for both of us.”

Cyrus stared at the wall in shock as if to say, “Are you hearing this madness?”

The wall didn’t respond. It was only a wall after all.

“I’m going to be wed with an aetherin? I could never marry one of those vile beasts! They’re repulsive! Father, how could you agree to this?”

From what he heard of Dahrian, she was dreadful. She seldom went outside, and when she did, she was always either wielding a sword or in town with the blacksmith’s daughter. And Cyrus never noticed anything about her physical appearance or beauty because he was always distracted by those blasted wings on the side of her head. He shuddered. He would have to be seeing those ears for the rest of his life, apparently.

Bollocks. I should’ve used a skunk.

“Lord Brown said she was a bright young lady and was very charming. Now, I’ve never met the creature, but I’m sure she’s not completely unfortunate looking.”

Cyrus didn’t respond. He was already plotting how he could get out of this. He could… run away! Alas. That didn’t work the last time he tried. Maybe he could beg? Plead? Never mind. He was twenty-one years old, and though he was fairly immature, he would never stoop down to that level. He could fake his own death, mayhaps? Nay. Too risky.

This might genuinely be the end of his freedom.

“I’m going into town to get some air,” Cyrus muttered, already rising and turning towards the back door. He needed a plan. A good plan. A plan that would work. His mind raced as he donned his jacket and cloak. The chilly autumn air was always biting in the early hours of morn.

“Alright. I have already alerted the guards to make sure you resist running away,” his father called after him. The sound of a slamming door responded.

The Oclen countryside was quiet at this time in the morning, and the sun was just peeking over the mountains with a warm hello. It was still quite dark as Cyrus left his family’s property and took the main road to town, but the sky was slowly morphing into a distant orange. The pine trees along the path danced slowly in the slight breeze, bending and dipping subtly to the wind’s will, commanded by an unheard melody. The tangy scent was almost painfully strong, and it certainly didn’t help that the air around him was as cold as the ice beneath his boots. Snow flurries spun around him, playing with his dark blonde locks (which were in fact spun like fine gold, Dear Reader) and stinging his cheeks and nose a brilliant pink. He pulled at the collar of his jacket, trying to gain a little warmth and protection from the frigid breeze. Every breath was a cloud of white in front of him.

To take his mind off the cruel weather, he pondered the news that brought him out here in the first place. His mind raced as he thought about the future. He didn’t know much about romance, just what he read in books. Chivalry. Holding hands. Occasionally rescuing a hapless damsel from the greedy clutches of a lesser dragon. And of course, true love.

Ew?

Hmm.

He wasn’t fully sure what he felt about love yet.

He wandered down the path with romance on his mind and was entirely lost in his thoughts, which was a mistake. He collided straight into a girl.

“Ah!” she yelped, stumbling back into the snow.

“My most humble apology! I didn’t see you,” Cyrus stammered, the words tripping and tumbling as he spoke. The sun carved its golden way through the pine needles, revealing the maiden. Her blue eyes met his from under the hood of a cloak, and he felt his heart jump. Her heart-shaped face broke the awkwardness with a small smile. Like the dawn broke the dark. There was also something oddly familiar about her that he couldn’t quite put his finger on. Maybe he had seen her around town?

“Here, let me help you up,” Cyrus said, holding out a hand to the lady. “I am incredibly sorry.”

She took his outstretched hand and he pulled her out of the snowbank.

“‘Tis fine. I wasn’t watching my step either,” she said, smoothing her dress. Her voice had an airy and gentle quality to it, like birdsong in the spring.

She was also probably the most beautiful woman Cyrus had ever seen in his whole twenty-one years of living. Her skin was pale but not sickly, more like satin than the snow around them. Dark curls of hair framed her face and pooled from under her hood around her collarbone. Her cheeks flushed as Cyrus realised he was still holding her hand. He let go, clearing his throat.

“What is a fair maiden like thyself doing out here this early in the morn?” he asked, attempting to break the silence. The smile vanished from her mouth, and she seemed hesitant for a moment.

“I… I just needed some air.”

“Ah, yes, of course. Air. A noble pursuit indeed.”

Oh dear. Why did you say that? Cyrus demanded to himself. There is no possible response to that answer. She must think you’re a fool with no social skills.

Cyrus was, in fact, a fool with no social skills.

“And what are you doing out here, good sir? Mysteriously wandering the morning roads before dawn?”

“Mysterious? Me? Nay, I was merely… um…” He scrambled for a quick reason. “Reflecting. A man needs time to think.”

“About what?” She seemed genuinely curious.

“Well,” he began. It was his turn to hesitate now. “My family has decided to wed me against my will.”

For a moment, the maiden had a strange look in her eyes. He really wished he could figure out who this girl was. “Really?” she asked, the smile returning to her lips, which were either the color of carnelians or roses. Cyrus couldn’t decide, but he knew he should probably stop looking at her lips. “I thought young lads liked to be married off.”

“Well, I got married to a horrid wretch. I would have preferred a pretty maiden, but alas, I did not get to choose,” he said with a small chuckle. “Anyways, I long for freedom more than anything else.”

“I see,” she said, her eyes colder than before.

“And what about you, m’lady? Is air truly your pursuit, or are you also avoiding something?” He tried to give her a smile or at least appear a little charming, but his face didn’t seem to be working right. It was probably the cold.

She pursed her lips. Carnations, perhaps. A little more pink than red. “I’m also being married against my will. I didn’t get to choose him, so he’s some self-righteous prat that my parents picked.”

“Do you jest?”

She shook her head, and the slight grin returned once more. Cyrus assumed he was having some sort of heart issue because his cardiovascular system felt as if it were plummeting off a cliff and then leaping to the stars. He probably needed a good bleeding.

“That’s rather unfortunate,” he said.

“Indeed.”

As he looked into the maiden’s eyes, their sapphire hue almost bewitching him, he felt as if he had never had a deeper connection with another person in his entire life. Especially a pretty maiden. As the sun rose, he felt something stir in him. A feeling deep in his chest. Is this love? The two exchanged small smiles again, and his heart fluttered. He wanted to take her delicate hand and give her a twirl. This was the girl he wanted to marry if he had the choice. Not Dahrian Brown with her creepy wings. Maybe he could change his father’s mind now that he was finally willing to give up his title of bachelor for this lady. At that moment, Cyrus took a quick deep breath. He decided to take a risk.

He held out his hand once more. “Well, while we are both still unmarried, shall I take thee to town, m’lady?”

She blinked, and the sunlight was caught in her lashes. Cyrus caught a brief emotion that crossed her face. Alarm. Then, it quickly vanished, and he wasn’t sure if he had actually seen it.

“Sure,” she gave him another lop-sided smile and took his arm.

As the sky turned to a peachy blue, they strolled to town, discussing silly pleasantries and other things. When the two finally made it to the Square, the maiden waved goodbye, turned, and disappeared into the crowd.

That’s when Cyrus realised his terrible mistake.

He didn’t get her name.

“M’lady!” he called after her, desperately searching for a deep red cloak amongst the sea of commoners. “I didn’t catch thy name!”

But there was no response from his new acquaintance.

As the young maiden passed by the flower shop, she ducked behind the stand, finally out of sight of the man she had just met. She sighed in relief as she took off her hood. Her winged ears quivered from being forced to sit still so long, and her hands trembled from fear. That was close. Too close.

She risked a glance over the counter.

So that was Cyrus Grenwood.
